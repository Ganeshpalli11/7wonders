TAJ MAHAL

The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum on the south bank of the Yamuna river in the Indian city of Agra. It was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor, Shah Jahan (reigned from 1628 to 1658), to house the tomb of his favourite wife, Mumtaz Mahal. The tomb is the centrepiece of a 17-hectare (42-acre)complex, which includes a mosque and a guest house, and is set in formal gardens bounded on three sides by a crenellated wall. Construction of the mausoleum was essentially completed in 1643 but work continued on other phases of the project for another 10 years. The Taj Mahal complex is believed to have been completed in its entirety in 1653 at a cost estimated at the time to be around 32 million rupees, which in 2015 would be approximately 52.8 billion rupees (U.S. $827 million). The construction project employed some 20,000 artisans under the guidance of a board of architects led by the court architect to the emperor, Ustad Ahmad Lahauri. GREAT CHINA WALL

The Great Wall of China, a revered marvel ranked among the Seven Wonders of the World, stretches majestically across the northern frontiers of China. Its awe-inspiring saga commenced around the 7th century BC, with its construction and fortification spanning centuries and dynasties, reaching its zenith during the Ming Dynasty (1368‚Äì1644 AD). Crafted from an array of materials including stone, brick, and earth, this architectural symphony wasn't a singular structure but a complex amalgamation of walls, watchtowers, and fortifications. While its status as visible from space is a myth, its significance remains indelible. Tourists flock to its renowned stretches like Badaling and Mutianyu, not only for the breathtaking views but to also immerse in China's rich historical narrative, where the wall stood as an unwavering guardian against nomadic incursions. Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Great Wall of China stands as an enduring emblem of human endeavor and a living testament to the historical chapters that have shaped our world.

THE COLOSSEUM

The Colosseum, an epitome of ancient Rome's architectural grandeur, proudly stands in the heart of Rome, Italy. Built between AD 72 and AD 80 under Emperor Vespasian and completed by his successor Titus, this monumental amphitheater, known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, boasts an elliptical design accommodating up to 80,000 spectators. A testament to Roman engineering ingenuity, the Colosseum was a center of entertainment, hosting an array of events from gladiatorial combat and animal hunts to mock sea battles. Its engineering brilliance is evident in the intricate network of underground passages and elevators that facilitated dramatic entrances and exits. Adorned with statues and decorative elements, the Colosseum reflects the opulence of the Roman Empire. Despite facing natural disasters and damage, it endures as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, inviting modern visitors to relive the echoes of ancient history within its hallowed walls, as it stands as a timeless embodiment of Rome's cultural legacy and architectural mastery.

CHRIST THE REDEEMER

Christ the Redeemer, an iconic symbol of faith and a masterpiece of artistry, graces the cityscape of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. This colossal statue of Jesus Christ, standing atop the Corcovado mountain, was conceived by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and French sculptor Paul Landowski. Constructed between 1922 and 1931, this towering monument reaches a height of 98 feet (30 meters), with its outstretched arms spanning an impressive 92 feet (28 meters). A symbol of Christianity and a tribute to Brazil's religious devotion, the statue offers panoramic vistas of the vibrant city and its stunning surroundings. Embodying the harmony of faith and human achievement, Christ the Redeemer has earned its rightful place as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, attracting countless visitors who are captivated not only by its sheer size but also by the spiritual essence it radiates. Standing sentinel over Rio, this monumental sculpture etches its legacy as a testament to both artistic brilliance and unwavering devotion.

MACHU PICCHU

Machu Picchu, a testament to ancient Incan ingenuity and an enigma of archaeological wonder, is nestled high within the Andes Mountains of Peru. This intricate city, perched at an altitude of approximately 7,970 feet (2,430 meters), was constructed during the 15th century under the reign of Inca emperor Pachacuti. Its precise purpose remains a subject of debate, with theories ranging from a royal estate to a religious sanctuary. The site's awe-inspiring architecture includes meticulously cut stone walls, terraced fields, and sophisticated drainage systems that showcase the Incas' mastery of engineering and construction. Abandoned and hidden from the Spanish conquistadors, Machu Picchu remained largely unknown until its rediscovery in 1911 by Hiram Bingham. Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, this mystical citadel draws visitors from around the world to marvel at its breathtaking vistas and ponder its historical mysteries. As one gazes upon the sacred ruins and the surrounding natural beauty, Machu Picchu whispers tales of a civilization that once thrived in the heights of the Andes, leaving an indelible mark on the tapestry of human history.
